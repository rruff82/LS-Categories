Example 3.1.2. Exercise 2.

Suppose \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) and \(B \xrightarrow{k} A\) are both inverses...
Solution.
By definition, \(B \xrightarrow{g} A\) being an inverse implies \(g \circ f = 1_A\) and \(f \circ g = 1_B\text{.}\) Likewise, \(B \xrightarrow{k} A\) being an inverse implies \(k \circ f = 1_A\) and \(f \circ k = 1_B\text{.}\)
Suppose that \(g \neq k\text{.}\) There would need to exist some \(y \in B\) with \(g(y) \neq k(y)\text{.}\) Denote these values \(x_1 = g(y)\) and \(x_2 = k(y)\) for some \(x_1,x_2 \in A\) with \(x_1 \neq x_2\text{.}\)
Consider the point: \((k \circ f)(x_1)\text{.}\) Since \(k \circ f = 1_A\text{,}\) we must have \((k \circ f)(x_1) = 1_A(x_1) = x_1\text{.}\) However, since \(x_1 = g(y)\) we can also write this as \((k \circ f)(x_1)=(k \circ f)(g(y))\text{.}\) By the associative property, \((k \circ f)(g(y))=k(f(g(y))) = k((f \circ g)(y)) = k(1_B(y)) = k(y) = x_2\text{.}\) Having shown that \((k \circ f)(x_1)=x_1\) and \((k \circ f)(x_1)=x_2\text{,}\) this contradicts our earlier assumption that \(x_1 \neq x_2\) and completes our proof that \(g=k\) by contradiction.
