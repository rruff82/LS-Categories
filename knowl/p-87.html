Consider the point: \((k \circ f)(x_1)\text{.}\) Since \(k \circ f = 1_A\text{,}\) we must have \((k \circ f)(x_1) = 1_A(x_1) = x_1\text{.}\) However, since \(x_1 = g(y)\) we can also write this as \((k \circ f)(x_1)=(k \circ f)(g(y))\text{.}\) By the associative property, \((k \circ f)(g(y))=k(f(g(y))) = k((f \circ g)(y)) = k(1_B(y)) = k(y) = x_2\text{.}\) Having shown that \((k \circ f)(x_1)=x_1\) and \((k \circ f)(x_1)=x_2\text{,}\) this contradicts our earlier assumption that \(x_1 \neq x_2\) and completes our proof that \(g=k\) by contradiction.
