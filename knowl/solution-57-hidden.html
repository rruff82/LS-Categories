Our basic idea here is that \(f\) "respects the structure" of our category \(\mathcal{S}^{\circlearrowright}\text{.}\) That is:
\begin{equation*} \boxed{X^{\circlearrowright \alpha}} \overset{f}{\longrightarrow} \boxed{Y^{\circlearrowright \beta}} \end{equation*}
Satisifies \(\boxed{f \circ \alpha = \beta \circ f}\) for all endomaps \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\)
Now suppose we have another map \(g\) with
\begin{equation*} \boxed{Y^{\circlearrowright \beta}} \overset{g}{\longrightarrow} \boxed{Z^{\circlearrowright \gamma}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \boxed{g \circ \beta = \gamma \circ g} \end{equation*}
We can then compose these maps to produce a composition \(g \circ f\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \boxed{X^{\circlearrowright \alpha}} \overset{g \circ f}{\longrightarrow} \boxed{Z^{\circlearrowright \gamma}} \end{equation*}
This map should preserve structure also if we can prove that \((g \circ f) \circ \alpha = \gamma (g \circ f)\text{.}\) This should be fairly straight forward to show using the associative property and our previous assumptions:
\begin{equation*} (g \circ f) \circ \alpha = g \circ f \circ \alpha \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = g \circ (f \circ \alpha) = g \circ (\beta \circ f) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = (g \circ \beta) \circ f = (\gamma \circ g) \circ f \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \gamma \circ (g \circ f) \end{equation*}
And that completes the exercise.
