I think we can develop a similar definition of an "antipodal map" on the circle by reflecting across the center of the disk. This map \(C \xrightarrow{a} C\) is a unique endomap on \(C\text{.}\) It has no fixed points for the circle — \(\forall x \in C: a(x) \neq x\) — and acts as it's own inverse — \(a \circ a = 1_C\text{.}\) We can extend this map to the disk, but it fails to provide an inverse for the point at the center which we reflected over. Our antipodal map is the only possible section of the disk's boundary, but it fails to be a retraction due to this "missing" point in the middle.
